Lànîk

Introduction

Lànîk (la'nik) is the language of the elves on the island of Bésa (baysa), located in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of Japan. This document outlines the Lànîk language, how to use it, and all of its intricacies.

***

Elven vs Elf vs. Elvish

The difference between "elven" and "elvish" is quite simple: an elf is an elven being (the same way you're a human being), and if something is elvish then it's of elves.

"You're human, and I'm elven" -- Correct || "You're a human, and I'm an elven" -- Incorrect || "You're a human, and I'm an elf" -- Correct

Clear as mud, right? This concept is difficult for humans (especially English-speaking ones) because "human" is used for multiple means while "elven" and "elvish" are each only used for one. Basically, "elven" is only used to describe species, and "elvish" is used to describe origin, while "human" is used for both.

"You're a human being, a.k.a. a human, doing human things" vs. "I'm an elven being, a.k.a. an elf, doing elvish things."

Once you've wrapped your head around it, and with practice, knowing which to say will pretty much be up to what sounds right.

Language History

Lànîk is the modern Elvish language of the elves in Bésa, but before its development between 200 and 300 CE, elves spoke, wrote, and communicated in Ofrana (ofrana). The earliest known use of Ofrana was spoken and written in Bésa as far back as 200,000 BCE by homo elevensis, who would later become known as Elves. For reference, the homo elevensis split from its common ancestor with homo sapiens, the neanderthals, separating elven evolution from human evolution approximately 250,000 years ago.

Between 1667 and 1674, Ofrana’s written form, called Xinbo (sheenbo), was replaced with a latin script known as Ekdeçi (ekdeth'ee), as Xinbo’s unique script was widely considered difficult to learn and over-complicated.  This new form of writing naturally morphed the spoken language into Lanîk as we know it today such that is considered an entirely separate language. This was made official in 1762.

Today, elves in Bésa also speak other languages as well: English, being the most common language in business and media; Mandarin, the most commonly spoken language in the world; and Japanese because of its geographical proximity to Japan, as well as Japan’s many cultural similarities to Bésian culture.

As of the creation of this document, English is a mandatory part of Bésa’s national school curriculum, with daily classes in English beginning in komonida (komoneeda, preschool, ages 3–4), and continuing through sel-boken (sel-boken, ages 13–14). In dogako (dogako, secondary school, ages 12–13 through 17–18), English becomes an elective class, students can choose whether or not to take English classes.

In ai-boken (aee-boken, 1st grade, ages 5–6), students have the choice to learn Mandarin or Japanese in weekly classes, but must choose one or the other. In dogako, students can choose whether or not to continue taking them.

Through this aspect of the Bésian school system, by the time they sotsugo (sotsugo, graduate) from aigako (aee-gako, primary school), all elves are at least bilingual, able to speak Lanîk natively, English almost fluently, and can make basic conversation in either Mandarin or Japanese.

Diacritics

The Lànîk alphabet uses diacritics (or accents) on some of its letters to change their pronunciation in specific ways. These accents only apply to pre-determined letters, and cannot be used on other letters.

[ ¸ ]kistro, meaning “hook”used with Cc to form Çç
